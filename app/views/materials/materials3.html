Paint Cross-sections (+ rest)

What are paint cross-sections?

Cross-sections are minute paint samples that are carefully collected from an artwork.  They can provide important information on paint stratigraphy, pigments, coatings and later additions to original.  Ethical factors should always be considered when choosing a sampling location and weather to take a sample at all. Paint cross-sections have proved valuable when questions remain regarding an artwork’s material complexity, condition, authenticity, dating etc.

Preparing Cross-sections

Paint samples are usually taken along the edges of paintings or at the areas of cracks or losses in paint and ground layers. Tools for sampling include small brushes, tweezers and scalpels.  Good magnification, using head loupe or a microscope is often required.  The microscopic paint sample is then cast in a clear polyester resin together with an identification label and let to harden properly.  The resin is polished smooth using fine abrasives until the paint sample surface is reached within the resin block.  Ideally all ground and paint layers will be exposed during this process, providing the scientist or conservator with a “cross-sectional” view of the artwork’s stratigraphy.

Visible and Ultraviolet Light Microscopy

Paint cross-sections are typically viewed through a microscope that is ideally equipped with an ultraviolet light source. Samples are usually examined and photographed using reflected visible light and ultraviolet light as both methods can provide complementary information. Certain organic materials like animal glues and natural resins as well as some pigments will auto- fluoresce when exposed to ultraviolet light.  In addition, visual layer separation is better in most cases.

In case of Kumla altar, paint cross-sections were collected in order to gain better understanding on the use of pigments and layering technique, including glazes, used in painted surfaces of the altar. Samples were taken from two panels of the “Passion of the Christ” scene - “The Christ carrying the Cross” and “The Crist in Getsemane”, from the “Annunciation” scene - “The Angel Gabriel”, as well as from one sculpture - St. Olaf. The information gained from the paint cross- sections was compared to the results, obtained by using XRF (X-ray Fluorescence) and other analysis from the same areas of the painted surface.

Cross sections taken from the Kumla altar

Total of 13 cross-section samples were taken from the Kumla altar, 12 of them from the painted wings of the altar and one from the sculpture of St. Olov. Areas from where the sampling was done are presented in connection to each cross-section.

The microscope images of the cross-sections show the paint layer build-up as well as combination of different pigments used in each layer. The cross-sections were also analyzed under UV light and the images are included here.
