Punch work patterns

Punching or punch work is commonly understood as tooling of the gold or silver by the impression or stamping of patterns by means of a tool (punch), the tip of which is shaped with a figure or motif.

On the Kumla altar-piece there is punch work and impression technique used in many different decorated areas where gold and silver leaf has been used. In closer inspection, punch work patterns on gold and silver are giving an idea of the tools used to decorate the surface.

In the corpus and on both of the insides of the inner wings, the background is gilded on a thin chalk and animal glue ground. The technique used is polished water gilding. There are richly punched haloes in the gilded background for each of the sculptures, each and every one having different patterns. (Kuvat Haloes Corpus 1,2,3,4)(Kuvat Haloes Inner left wing 1,2,3,4) (Kuvat Haloes Inner Right wing 1,2,3,4)

Punched haloes, although in a more simple pattern can also be found in the Passion of Christ scenes, again stamped in to a polished water gilded background. (Kuvat Halos and punchwork other 1,2,3,4,)

Tools used to produce the punched and impressed patterns have most probably been 5 metal punches, having rather simple shapes. The shapes found in the decorated areas include a flower–shaped one with five petals, a larger as well as a smaller circle, a larger dot and a smaller dot.

Combining these five shapes, it has been possible to produce quite elaborate punched halo decorations for all sculptures as well as punched border decorations in the gilded backgrounds of the Passion of Christ scenes.

In addition, an impression-making tool, shaped like a wheel, possibly rotella, similar to one used for pricking the paper cartoon has been used to make lines consisting of minute dots. These can be found on silvered areas on soldiers´ armoury and helmets as well as part of the lines and delicate leaf motifs in the gilded backgrounds of the Passion of Christ scenes. There is a possibility that some of the tiny dotted lines and shapes have been also made by using a prick punch. (Kuvat Halos and punchwork other 5,6,7,8,9, 10,11)

Main round lines for haloes in between the punched patterns in the Kumla altar are probably made with the help of compass-like tool. (Kuva Halos and punchwork other 12)
