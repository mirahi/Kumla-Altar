Sculptures

The sculptures and parts of the altar-piece are described as they are seen by the viewer – when left and right is mentioned, the sculpture is always looked from the front.

Central motif in the corpus of the altar is the Coronation of the Virgin (kuva: the Coronation of the Virgin). The Virgin Mary is seated on a long profiled bench in the orant position, half turned towards Christ who raises right hand in blessing while the left rests on an orb on his left knee.

To the left of the central group, is the Holy Bishop St. Eskil, at right St. Torkel, the local patron saint of the Kumla church, wearing civilian clothes.

In left wing, inside, starting from the left stand St. Gertrude, St. Olov and closest to the corpus St. Peter.

In right wing, inside, starting from the right stand St. Birgitta as the farthest, then St. Erik and as a pendant to St Peter stands St. Paul.

The ten polychrome sculptures of the Kumla altar-piece are all carved and decorated in the same manner and also share same types of damages. Therefore the description of the materials and condition will be first presented generally for all sculptures, including timber used for carving and sculpture´s structure, metal hooks for hanging, linen and parchment used for joints in wood or weaker areas, surface/coatings: gesso, bole and gilding, silvering with glazing, Zwischgold (composite of gold and silver leaf, beaten together) and paint.

Only the differing information will be listed separately for each sculpture: measurements, missing parts, cracks in the timber, linen and parchment supports, paint, silvering and hooks. The sculptures also share same or similar types of paint layers with each other and therefore the paint layers are introduced generally.

Photos of the sculptures in the Kumla altar are accompanied with a short text, related to more detailed information about each sculpture.

General description of the sculptures and materials

Timber and structure

All wooden parts are made of oak. Most of the ten sculptures are carved from one piece of oak. Only some of the attributes and few other parts are carved as a separate piece and attached to the sculpture with wooden pegs and glue. Backs of the sculptures are hollowed and untreated. On the backs there are tool marks from both straight chisels and gouges (concave chisels) and a small axe. The width of the straight chisels vary between 1cm and 4 cm and the width of gouges vary between 1cm and 3,5 cm.

On top of each saints’ head there is the remains of a wooden peg. A hole has been first drilled to the top of each sculptures’ head and a peg has been inserted. The peg was traditionally used during the woodcarving as an aid; the sculpture could be rotated in the workbench during carving, gesso application, gilding and painting.

The peg helped to get a grip of the sculptures in order not to touch the finished areas. After the finishes were done the peg was sawn off leaving only a round area visible on the top of the head of the sculpture which usually doesn’t have any finishing layer on it.

Metal hooks

An iron hook has been attached to the timber from the back of the sculptures. A hole has been drilled first either from behind the head or in the hollowed back for the hook. With the help of the hook the sculptures are attached to the altar-piece. On the surface of the original hooks there is still some red lead paint left. It has most probably been applied on the metal in order to protect it from corroding.

Linen and parchment

During the carving, on areas where carving went too deep and the wood became too thin, also on top of wood knots and joints where extra pieces were added, a linen cloth or parchment was glued on the wood as an additional support for weaker areas and joints in the wood.

These kinds of supports were also added to prevent the finishing layers from flaking when the wood would shrink or crack while drying. The supporting parchment and linen cloth have been glued on top of the timber on the back side of the sculpture or to the front side before applying the gesso ground.

On the Virgin Mary and Christ figures, text in German can be seen on a small area of the parchment, which indicates that the parchment has been recycled from old manuscripts.

Surface/coatings

Gesso:

The ground is most probably a mixture of chalk and animal glue. It has been applied on all visible surfaces of the sculpture except for the bottom parts of the sculptures and on the backsides. The thickness of the layer varies in between different areas.

Bole:

There is a red bole layer on top of the gesso ground in areas where the gilding has been applied. It has been unevenly applied and in most areas there seems to be only one layer of it. The bole has not been covered by other finishing layers on areas that cannot be seen when looking at the sculptures from the front – for instance on the left side of the St. Gertrud sculpture.

Gilding:

Over 50% of each sculptures’ surface has been gilded. The outside of garments are mostly gilded and some of the attributes and their details are gilded as well. All gilding on the sculptures is water gilding. Silvering with Glazing: Silvering has been used together with coloured glazing on some of the attributes of the saints. As an example, silvering and glazing can be found on the body of the human - headed dragon, lying under St. Olov’s feet, on the walls and imitation stained glass windows of the church St. Gertrud is holding and in the Virgins hair.(kuvat: 4, 5)

Zwischgold:

Zwischgold has been applied on narrow areas of the gilded garment on the both sides of the sculptures, on the areas which are not visible when looking at the sculptures from the front. Zwischgold is a leaf of silver covered with a very thin layer of gold, beaten together. It has originally had a similar visual appearance as gold leaf but has been a cheaper material to use in areas that can hardly be seen after the altar was finished. Edges of the now partly oxidized leaves are clearly visible on for instance on the sides of St. Gertrud sculpture.

Paint:

The sculptures’ heads and faces with all the details are painted. There is also paint on the sculptures’ bases, attributes and linings of the garments as well as on the details of the clothing. All painted areas of the sculptures have only been painted once except the bases of the Virgin and Christ, where green layer of paint can be seen under the light red paint layer. No retouching has been done previously except one area in St. Olov´s left side of the garment. On several borderlines of paint and gilding, the paint has been applied also a little bit on top of the gilding. This indicates clearly that the sculptures have been painted only after gilding.

A short iconography of the Sculptures

Sculptures in he left wing

St. Gertrude: A German Benedictine nun saint who was born on the feast of the Epiphany, January 6, 1256.. In 1281, at the age of twenty-five, she experienced the first of a series of visions. Gertrude became one of the great mystics of the 13th century. Together with her friend and teacher St. Mechtild, she practiced a spirituality called "nuptial mysticism," that is, she came to see herself as the bride of Christ. Gertrude died at Helfta, near Eisleben, Saxony, around 1302.

St. Olov (995 – 29 July 1030): Patron saint of Norway, the king of Norway between the years of 1015 and 1028. Olaf's local canonisation was in 1164 confirmed by Pope Alexander III, making him a universally recognised saint of the Roman Catholic Church. He is also a canonised saint of the Eastern Orthodox Church (feast day celebrated July 29 (translation August 3)) and one of the last famous Western saints before the Great Schism. Olaf swiftly became Norway's patron saint; his canonisation was performed only a year after his death by Bishop Grimkell. The cult of Olaf not only unified the country, it also fulfilled the conversion of the nation, something for which the king had fought so hard. St. Olaf was widely popular not only in Norway but throughout Scandinavia. Numerous churches in Norway, Sweden, and Iceland were dedicated to him.

St. Peter:Also known as Simon Peter, Simeon, or Simōn, according to the New Testament. According to the New Testament, he was one of the Twelve Apostles of Jesus Christ, leaders of the early Christian Church. An apostle and martyr. The patron saint of many churches in Sweden.

Sculptures in the corpus

St. Eskil: (died 1069) Anglo-Saxon monk particularly venerated during the end of the 11th century in the Province of Södermanland, Sweden. He is the patron saint of Södermanland and the Diocese of Strängnäs. Saint Eskil made the village of Tuna (present day Eskilstuna) his missionary diocese and later, around 1080, he made a 30 kilometre journey east of Tuna to Strängnäs, an Old Norse holy place. Saint Eskil was stoned to death, according to tradition, because he disrupted a holy ritual.

The Virgin Mary: According to the New Testament, Mary, also known as Saint Mary, the Virgin Mary, Our Lady, and the Madonna, was a Galilean Jewish woman of Nazareth and the mother of Jesus. The gospels of Matthew and Luke in the New Testament describe Mary as a virgin and Christians believe that she conceived her son while a virgin by the Holy Spirit. This took place when she was already betrothed to Joseph and was awaiting the concluding rite of marriage, the formal home-taking ceremony. She married Joseph and accompanied him to Bethlehem, where Jesus was born. The Gospel of Luke begins its account of Mary's life with the Annunciation, when the Angel Gabriel appeared to her and announced her divine selection to be the mother of Jesus.

Christ: Son of the Virgin Mary. The Christ (/kraɪst/; Ancient Greek: Χριστός, Christós, meaning "anointed") is a translation of the Hebrew מָשִׁיחַ (Māšîaḥ) and the Syriac (M'shiha), the Messiah, and is used as a title for Jesus in the New Testament. Among Christians, "Christ" is treated as synonymous with Jesus of Nazareth. The followers of Jesus became known as Christians because they believed Jesus to be the Christós (from Greek, "Messiah") prophesied in the Greek Old Testament, for example in the Confession of Peter.

St. Torkel: (Torgils) From 1200-s. Local patron saint of Kumla church. Not much is known about this locally worshipped saint. A clue might come from the clocker staff in his hand (now missing from the sculpture in the Kumla altar) which might be the weapon he was martyred with.

Sculptures in he right wing

St. Paul: An apostle and martyr. Paul the Apostle (Greek: Παῦλος Paulos), originally known as Saul of Tarsus (Hebrew: שאול התרסי‎; Greek: Σαῦλος Ταρσεύς Saulos Tarseus),was an apostle (though not one of the Twelve Apostles) who taught the gospel of Christ to the first-century world. He is generally considered one of the most important figures of the Apostolic Age. The patron saint of many churches in Sweden.

St. Erik: Eric IX of Sweden, (Swedish: Erik Jedvardsson; Erik den helige; martyred in 1160.), also called Eric the Lawgiver, Erik the Saint, Eric the Holy, and, in Sweden, Sankt Erik, meaning Saint Eric, was a Swedish king c. 1155-60. No historical records of Eric have survived, and all information about him is based on later legends that were aimed at having him established as a saint.

St. Birgitta: Bridget of Sweden (1303 – 23 July 1373); born as Birgitta Birgersdotter, also Birgitta of Vadstena, or Saint Birgitta (Swedish: den heliga Birgitta), was a mystic and saint, and founder of the Bridgettines nuns and monks after the death of her husband of twenty years. Outside of Sweden, she was also known as the Princess of Nericia[1] and was the mother of Catherine of Vadstena. She is one of the six patron saints of Europe, together with Benedict of Nursia, Saints Cyril and Methodius, Catherine of Siena and Edith Stein. The only canonized saint from Sweden.
